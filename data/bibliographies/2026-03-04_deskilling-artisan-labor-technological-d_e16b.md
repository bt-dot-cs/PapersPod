# Annotated Bibliography: deskilling artisan labor technological displacement

As machines have historically reshaped the landscape of human labor, the question of who gains and who loses has never been straightforward. This episode explores how technological change doesn't simply replace workers but fundamentally restructures the value of their knowledge and craft — eroding hard-won expertise while simultaneously generating new, often narrowly defined skill demands. Drawing on economic theory, historical political upheaval, and patent-linked occupational data, we examine deskilling not as an inevitable byproduct of progress, but as a dynamic process with deep consequences for labor markets, social identity, and political belonging.

---

**Brugger, F., & Gehrke, C. (2018). Skilling and deskilling: Technological change in classical economic theory and its empirical evidence.** *European Journal of the History of Economic Thought, 25*(6), 1256–1296. https://doi.org/10.1080/09672567.2018.1523204

This article bridges intellectual history and empirical economic history by comparing how classical economists—Smith, Marx, and Babbage—theorized the skill effects of technological change with what historical evidence actually shows about labor markets during industrialization. A key methodological contribution is the paper's attention to *inducement mechanisms*, meaning the economic and social incentives that drive firms to adopt skill-biased or deskilling technologies; here, the authors find stronger empirical support for Marx's class-conflict hypothesis than for Smith's more neutral "division of labor" account. Rather than confirming a clean deskilling narrative, the historical evidence points toward *labor polarization*—simultaneous deskilling of many workers alongside rising demand for a smaller number of high-skilled workers—a finding that resonates with contemporary debates about technology and labor market inequality.

---

**Alsan, M., Eriksson, K., & Niemesh, G. T. (2020). *Understanding the success of the Know-Nothing Party*.** https://doi.org/10.3386/w28078

Using historical voting data from Massachusetts, the authors employ a difference-in-differences style analysis to link local exposure to Irish immigrant labor — specifically labor market crowding and manufacturing deskilling — to elevated Know-Nothing vote shares in the 1855 state elections. The study finds that native workers facing wage and occupational displacement were more likely to support the nativist party, though occupational upgrading partially offset wealth losses, complicating a straightforward economic grievance narrative. This paper engages with broader debates on the economic roots of nativist political movements, offering a historical case study that speaks to contemporary discussions about immigration, labor market competition, and populist backlash.

---

**Kogan, L., Papanikolaou, D., Schmidt, L., & Seegmiller, B. (2021). *Technology, vintage-specific human capital, and labor displacement: Evidence from linking patents with occupations.* Working paper.**

This paper constructs a novel measure of workers' exposure to new technologies by using text analysis to match patent documents with the task descriptions of specific occupations, offering a more granular approach than standard industry-level automation metrics. The authors find a nuanced displacement pattern using administrative earnings data: rather than affecting only low-wage routine workers, technological change also suppresses earnings growth for top earners whose specialized skills become obsolete—a mechanism they attribute to "vintage-specific human capital," meaning that workers' expertise is tied to particular technologies that may lose value over time. The study contributes to ongoing debates about technology and inequality by emphasizing that changes in the *quantities* of skills employed in the labor market, not just shifts in how skills are priced, are critical for understanding how innovation affects wage distributions.
