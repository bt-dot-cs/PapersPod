# Deskilling Artisan Labor Technological Displacement — PapersPod

**Alex:** Here's a question worth sitting with: when a machine replaces a skilled worker, what exactly gets lost? Not just the job — but the knowledge, the identity, the social standing that came with mastering a craft. And here's what makes this urgent right now — we're having this exact debate again with AI and automation, but the economic history of deskilling goes back centuries, and we've actually learned a lot from it. Today we're digging into three papers that together build a surprisingly coherent story about how technological change restructures skill, who bears the cost, and — maybe most fascinatingly — what happens politically when those costs fall unevenly.

**Jordan:** And I think what drew me into this topic is that word 'deskilling' itself. It sounds simple — technology makes your skills obsolete — but I'm guessing it's actually way more complicated than that?

**Alex:** Exactly right, and the best place to start is a 2018 paper by Florian Brugger and Christian Gehrke in the European Journal of the History of Economic Thought. They do something methodologically unusual: they go back to the classical economists — Adam Smith, Karl Marx, Charles Babbage — and ask, what did these thinkers actually predict about the skill effects of industrialization? And then they check those predictions against the empirical historical record. It's intellectual history meets economic history.

**Jordan:** So they're basically grading the classical economists on their predictions?

**Alex:** In a sense, yes. And the results are interesting. Smith's division of labor argument is relatively neutral on skill — he thought specialization would create efficiency but didn't have a strong theory about whether workers would end up more or less skilled overall. Babbage, who was deeply interested in machinery, saw deskilling as more or less inevitable as tasks got subdivided. But Marx — and this is where it gets empirically rich — Marx argued that deskilling wasn't just a technical outcome, it was driven by what Brugger and Gehrke call 'inducement mechanisms.' Firms actively chose deskilling technologies because it gave them leverage over workers. It was a class-conflict hypothesis, not just a technological inevitability.

**Jordan:** And the historical evidence? Who was right?

**Alex:** This is where it gets genuinely surprising. The authors find stronger empirical support for Marx's class-conflict framing than for the more neutral Smith account. But the headline finding isn't a clean deskilling story at all — it's labor polarization. Simultaneous deskilling of many workers alongside rising demand for a smaller number of highly skilled workers. Which, of course, maps directly onto what labor economists are documenting in contemporary data. So you have this resonance across centuries.

**Jordan:** Okay, so the theory side is rich. But how do we actually measure deskilling empirically in historical contexts? That seems like a data nightmare.

**Alex:** It is, and that's where our second paper takes a very different methodological approach. Alsan, Eriksson, and Niemesh — a 2020 NBER working paper — aren't primarily trying to measure deskilling directly. They're asking: what happens politically when deskilling hits a local labor market? Their setting is Massachusetts in the 1850s, during a massive wave of Irish immigration driven by the famine. They use town-level regression analysis — essentially a difference-in-differences design — linking local exposure to Irish immigrant labor with vote shares for the Know-Nothing Party in the 1855 state elections.

**Jordan:** The Know-Nothings — that was the nativist, anti-immigrant party of the era, right?

**Alex:** Exactly. Virulently anti-Catholic, anti-Irish. And what the authors find is that native workers in towns with more labor market crowding from Irish immigrants — particularly in manufacturing, where deskilling was most acute — were significantly more likely to vote Know-Nothing. The mechanism they're probing is economic grievance: displacement and wage compression pushing workers toward nativist politics. But here's the complication — they also find evidence of occupational upgrading among some native workers, meaning some natives moved into higher-skilled roles as Irish immigrants filled the bottom of the labor market. That partially offset the wealth losses.

**Jordan:** So it's not a simple story of 'deskilling causes nativism.'

**Alex:** Right, and that's what makes the paper methodologically careful. They're trying to disentangle the economic grievance channel from other potential drivers — cultural anxiety, religious antipathy. The town-level variation is crucial there. And it speaks to a much broader contemporary debate about the economic roots of populist backlash. Whether you're talking about Brexit or the Rust Belt, this paper offers a rigorous historical analog.

**Jordan:** Now, both of those papers are working in historical settings with somewhat indirect measures. Is there a paper in our set that gets closer to the micro-level mechanism of how technology actually hits individual workers?

**Alex:** Yes — and this is where the third paper really shines. Kogan, Papanikolaou, and Schmidt, published in 2021, do something genuinely novel. They link patent data to occupational classifications using text-based matching — essentially reading patent descriptions and asking, which occupations do these technologies most directly affect? Then they connect that to administrative panel data tracking individual worker earnings over time. What they're measuring is what they call 'vintage-specific human capital' — the idea that your skills aren't just generic, they're tied to a specific technological vintage. When that vintage becomes obsolete, your human capital depreciates, even if you're still employed.

**Jordan:** That's a subtle but really important distinction. It's not just about losing your job — it's about the value of what you know eroding underneath you.

**Alex:** Precisely. And their calibrated economic model lets them separate two things that are often conflated: the quantity of labor in an occupation versus the price of that labor. They find that technological exposure primarily hits earnings — wages fall — before employment actually drops. So workers experience a slow-burn displacement, not an abrupt layoff. This actually cuts against some of the more dramatic automation narratives that focus on mass unemployment.

**Jordan:** Does that finding contradict anything from the other papers?

**Alex:** In an interesting way, yes. Brugger and Gehrke's historical analysis, following the polarization literature, emphasizes the bifurcation of the skill distribution — you get winners and losers, not gradual erosion across the board. But Kogan et al. are finding that earnings displacement is quite widespread and gradual, hitting workers in exposed occupations broadly rather than carving out a polarized structure. Now, these are different historical periods and different methodological lenses, so it's not a clean contradiction — but it does highlight that the mechanism of displacement may look quite different depending on whether you're measuring wages, employment, or political outcomes.

**Jordan:** So putting these three together — what's the picture? And what questions do they leave open?

**Alex:** The picture is that deskilling is real, historically persistent, and politically consequential — but it's not a single uniform process. The theoretical framework from Brugger and Gehrke tells us incentives matter: firms don't just drift toward deskilling, they're induced to choose it. Alsan et al. show us the political fallout is real and measurable, even when labor markets partially adapt. And Kogan et al. give us the most granular look at how earnings erode before workers even know their occupation is at risk. What remains open? We still don't have a great unified framework for predicting which technological vintages will cause gradual wage erosion versus sharp structural displacement. And the political economy question — under what conditions does economic grievance translate into nativism versus collective bargaining versus policy reform — that's very much unsettled. History gives us cases, but the causal identification is always contested.

**Jordan:** Which sounds like exactly the kind of problem that needs more empirical work — and probably more conversations like this one. Thanks, Alex. And thanks to everyone listening to PapersPod.
